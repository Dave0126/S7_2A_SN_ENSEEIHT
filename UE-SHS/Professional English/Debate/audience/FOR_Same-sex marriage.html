Argument Laurent: Security issues

The pinnacle of self-driving car technology is a system in which the user does not have to interact with the vehicle's operation at all (the ability to manually steer and throttle may not even be given to him). To achieve this autonomy, powerful algorithms, engineering, and computers will be required. Currently, all self-driving vehicles use various sensors such as LIDARS, lasers, and cameras to gather information about their surroundings. With this information, and with the help of machine learning, they are able to make decisions on how to get from point A to point B. With such modifications, it would be almost inappropriate to refer to them as cars, we should rather talk about computers with wheels.
And like any computer connected to a network, it is vulnerable. Not only is it vulnerable to security issues (meaning an entity could take control of your car, as it was proven in 2019 by Amat Cama and Richard Zhu when they hacked a telsa by taking advantage of a flaw in the car's built-in internet browser), but it's also vulnerable in other interesting ways. As mentioned earlier, self-driving cars currently rely on computer vision algorithms trained from millions of hours of driving. Like any machine learning model, they are biased, which means they can be exploited in some weir ways. For example, in February 2020, hackers were able to get Tesla cars to reach 85 mph instead of the normal 35 mph, simply by taping off the speed sign a bit, to transform the 3 into an 8. We could also talk about how they sometimes confuse Burger King signs for stop signs, but I find it more interesting to talk about another aspect of security, privacy. In order to continuously evolve, self-driving cars record an absurd amount of data, such as itineraries, location, user inputs... The use of this data is currently not well regulated in most countries, and this data could be used maliciously, by insurers, governments or even terrorists. Also, you may have noticed it, but data leaks are becoming more common, and this puts users at risk far beyond car accidents (such as identity thef). That's why this house believes it's too early for autonomous vehicles.

There is a very famous 'tram dilemma', which is a metaphor for the fact that humans, as highly intelligent beings, will make choices that are more in line with their own moral standards when a moral dilemma arises. Immediately afterwards, some media outlets began to ask questions about autonomous driving. Let's say you are in a self-driving car driving down a wide, high-speed road and 10 people appear ahead of you. At this point the Autopilot system calculates that even if it brakes it will still inevitably hit the pedestrians in front of you, and it also calculates that quickly adjusting the front of the car to hit the adjacent barrier will effectively reduce the speed of the car and save the lives of the pedestrians in front of you, but may endanger you in the vehicle.
